Pink Floyd

Animals

Though initially overshadowed by its predecessors, Animals has grown into a fan favorite, praised for its bold social commentary and musical intensity, solidifying Pink Floyd's legacy in progressive rock.

About the Album

Released on January 23, 1977, Animals is Pink Floyd's tenth studio album, offering a scathing critique of societal hierarchy and capitalism. Inspired by George Orwell's Animal Farm, the album categorizes people into metaphorical animals—dogs (ruthless businessmen), pigs (corrupt leaders), and sheep (blind followers).
The album features extended progressive rock compositions such as “Dogs,” “Pigs (Three Different Ones),” and “Sheep.” It marks a shift towards a harder, more guitar-driven sound, with David Gilmour's soaring solos and Roger Waters' biting lyrics taking center stage.
Recorded at Britannia Row Studios and produced by Pink Floyd, Animals is known for its raw energy, politically charged themes, and unique song structures. The album cover, designed by Hipgnosis, features a giant inflatable pig floating above Battersea Power Station, an image that has since become an enduring symbol of the band.
